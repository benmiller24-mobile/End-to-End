# Professional Kitchen Design: Upper Cabinet Alignment & Countertop Placement

Research compiled from NKBA guidelines, cabinet manufacturers (KraftMaid, Dura Supreme, CliqStudios, Dean Cabinetry, Fabuwood), stone fabricators, Fine Homebuilding, and professional kitchen-design firms. Every rule below gives the concrete quantitative value and a one-line rationale. The final section translates these into codifiable solver/renderer constraints.

---

## PART 1 — WALL (UPPER) CABINET ALIGNMENT

### 1.1 Aligning uppers with the base cabinets below

- **Default: upper cabinet vertical edges align with the base cabinet edges below them.** Wall cabinet widths are chosen to match the base cabinets below for the most balanced appearance. *Why: vertical seams that stack create visual order; misaligned seams read as accidental.*
- **Standard widths are a shared 3" module** for both uppers and bases (9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24, 27, 30, 33, 36, 42"), which makes edge-stacking achievable. *Why: a common increment lets the designer line up door seams up the wall.*
- **Fillers (≤3") absorb wall-length discrepancies** so the run still terminates cleanly at walls/appliances. Example: a 100.5" wall with 98" of cabinet gets a 2.5" filler. *Why: walls are rarely plumb/square, and fillers prevent binding and visible gaps without breaking the module.*

**When uppers intentionally do NOT align with the bases below:**
- **Over the sink:** the sink base is often the widest cabinet (33–36"), but the upper above it (or the window above it) drives a different width; centering on the sink/window wins over edge-matching the base. *Why: the sink/window is a focal point and must be visually centered.*
- **At the range/hood:** the hood must be ~6" wider than the cooktop for capture and "breathing room," so the flanking uppers will not align with the wider range base below. *Why: ventilation performance and proportion around the focal hood override edge-stacking.*
- **Specialty base components** (narrow spice pull-out, tray/cookie-sheet cabinet) throw off alignment by design. *Why: function of the base unit is allowed to break the grid in a localized spot.*
- **Priority rule (designers' consensus):** when you cannot have both, **make the UPPERS symmetric/consistent and let the bases be irregular** — the eye tracks horizontally at eye level, so irregular base widths are far less noticeable than irregular uppers.

### 1.2 Uppers aligning with EACH OTHER

- **Consistent BOTTOM line (height AFF) across a run is the single most important alignment rule.** Standard bottom of uppers sits **54" AFF** (36" base + 1.5" counter + ~16.5–18" backsplash gap). *Why: a continuous bottom sightline is what the eye reads as "level"; a stepped bottom looks like an error.*
- **Consistent TOP line.** If cabinets of different heights are used in one run, **hold the TOPS level and drop the shorter cabinet's bottom**, OR hold the bottoms level and let tops vary — but pick one datum and keep it. Most designers hold the **top line** when cabinets stack to crown/ceiling, and hold the **bottom line** for single-row uppers. *Why: two competing datums (top and bottom both varying) produces visual chaos.*
- **Over-sink / over-range exception:** a shorter cabinet over the sink or range may be raised so its bottom is **30–36" above the sink/cooktop** (vs. 18" elsewhere). To keep the top line continuous, that shorter cabinet (e.g., 24" or 30" tall) is hung with its top matching the neighbors. *Why: headroom/clearance and (over gas) fire clearance; the top line preserves order.*

### 1.3 Symmetry around focal points

- **Flank the hood with EQUAL-WIDTH uppers.** Equal cabinets left and right of the hood centerline. *Why: symmetrical balance around the kitchen's primary focal point.*
- **Center uppers on the window/sink, not on the base.** Maintain **equal reveal from each window edge** to the adjacent cabinet. *Why: consistent window-to-cabinet gap reads as intentional; centering on the base instead can leave the window visibly off-center.*
- **Asymmetric balance is acceptable** when true symmetry is impossible: balance a tall pantry on one side with open shelving / glass cabinet of equal visual weight on the other. *Why: equal visual weight, not mirror geometry, is the real goal.*

### 1.4 Cabinet over the refrigerator & over the hood

- **Over-fridge cabinet: 24" deep** (vs. 12" standard uppers) to align its face with the refrigerator case and adjacent tall/pantry cabinets. Width 30/33/36" to match the fridge enclosure; height typically 12–18" (single) or full-height to ceiling. *Why: a 12" cabinet floating over a 30–36" deep fridge looks unfinished and wastes a deep, useful zone; 24" creates a flush built-in face.*
- **Over-hood cabinet:** depth matched to the hood/flue (often 18–24"); the **bottom of the over-hood cabinet sits 66–72" AFF** so the hood has its required clearance below. *Why: keeps the flue concealed and the top line continuous while preserving cooktop clearance.*
- **Tall-cabinet depth staggering:** when mixing standard (12") and tall/deep cabinets in one elevation, tall cabinets are 3–15" deeper so crown of the shallower run can die cleanly into the tall cabinet's side. *Why: lets moldings terminate without blocking door swings.*

### 1.5 Single uppers + crown vs. stacked uppers; standard heights by ceiling

Standard single upper heights: **30", 36", 42".** Bottom of uppers at 54" AFF (18" above a 36" counter).

| Ceiling height | Recommended approach | Result |
|---|---|---|
| 8' (96") | **30" uppers** + crown, bottom at 54" AFF → top at 84", 12" to ceiling for crown/soffit | Single row; clean crown reveal |
| 9' (108") | **42" uppers** (top at 96", ~12" for crown) **OR** stacked 36"+ a 10–12" glass topper | Either fills the wall cleanly |
| 9' (108") | Avoid bare 30" uppers (would leave ~24" awkward gap) | 36" minimum if single row |
| 10' (120") | **Stacked uppers to ceiling** (e.g., 42" main + 24–30" upper tier) | Floor-to-ceiling; no dead wall |

- *Why stack:* above ~9' a single row leaves a large dead band of wall; stacking (or going to ceiling with crown) keeps proportion and adds storage.
- **Crown scale tracks ceiling:** 9' → 6–12" crown; 10' → 9–18" crown. *Why: molding must be proportionate to the wall height it caps.*

### 1.6 Mounting height (the 18" rule)

- **Standard: 18" of clear space from finished countertop to bottom of upper cabinet** (puts bottom of uppers at ~54" AFF over a 36" counter). *Why: clears most small appliances/backsplash and keeps the first shelf reachable.*
- **Vary it when:** over a sink/range raise to **30–36" above the appliance** for headroom and clearance; for shorter primary users some designers drop to **15"**; tall users / tall ceilings may go to 20–24". *Why: ergonomic reach vs. clearance trade-off.*

### 1.7 Glass-door & open-shelf placement

- **Place glass/open units symmetrically** — most commonly **one on each end of a run** or **flanking a focal point** (hood, window, range). *Why: symmetry; bookends frame the solid run between them.*
- **Keep glass/open units away from the cooking/splatter zone** (over counters/island, not directly beside the cooktop). *Why: grease and splatter foul open contents and glass interiors.*

### 1.8 Consistent reveals / spacing between cabinets

- **Inset (face-frame) doors: 1/8" (≈2–3 mm) reveal on all sides, held identical everywhere.** *Why: inset doors are boxed on all four sides so the eye catches the smallest inconsistency.*
- **Frameless (full-overlay): 3 mm gap between adjacent doors** (some shops use 2 mm door gap for a 4 mm reveal). *Why: hardware tolerance plus a crisp, even shadow line.*
- **Equal reveals against walls/appliances** along the whole elevation. *Why: consistent gaps are the difference between custom-looking and DIY-looking.*

## PART 2 — COUNTERTOP PLACEMENT & ALIGNMENT

### 2.1 Overhang past the base cabinet face

- **Front overhang: 1" to 1.5" past the cabinet door/face** (1.5" most common). *Why: protects door/drawer faces from drips and crumbs; <1" lets liquid run onto cabinet fronts.*
- **Side (exposed end) overhang: ~1–1.5"** to mirror the front. *Why: visual consistency on a finished end.*
- **Back: 0" (counter dies into the wall/backsplash).** *Why: backsplash seals the joint; no overhang needed.*
- **Standard finished counter depth: 25.5–26"** (24" cabinet box + 1.5" overhang). *Why: matches the standard module.*

### 2.2 Seating-side overhang by counter height

| Counter/eating height | Required seating overhang (clear knee space) |
|---|---|
| 30" (table height) | **18"** |
| 36" (counter height) | **15"** |
| 42" (bar height) | **12"** |

*Why: a taller stool means the sitter's knees bend less, so less knee clearance is needed. (Verified — the 30/36/42 → 18/15/12 mapping is the NKBA standard.)*
- **Support rule:** overhangs beyond ~10–12" of unsupported stone require corbels, brackets, or steel rod/plate. *Why: unsupported stone cantilever cracks under load/leaning.*

### 2.3 Seam placement

- **NEVER place a seam through a sink or cooktop cutout; keep seams ≥6" from any cutout edge.** *Why: cutouts are already the weakest, most unsupported zone; a seam there will separate or crack.*
- **A seam may be placed at the BACK edge of a cooktop cutout** where it aligns with the existing visual break. *Why: it hides behind the appliance and follows an existing line.*
- **A seam at the FRONT of a sink can work** because the eye stops at the sink rim. *Why: the rim visually terminates the line so the seam disappears.*
- **Prefer seams at inside corners of L/U layouts.** *Why: corners are natural break points and reduce stress on the stone.*
- **Place seams at the narrowest span, never across the widest/most-visible field.** *Why: shorter seam = less visible and less structurally stressed.*
- **Every seam must land over a supported cabinet wall/rail** (no light visible through the joint from below). *Why: unsupported seams crack the first time someone leans.*
- **Minimize seam count.** *Why: each seam is a visible line and a potential failure point.*
- **Quality tolerances:** seam width ≤1/16" (≈1.5 mm); lippage (height mismatch) ≤1/32".
- **Max slab length forces seams:** usable slab ≈ 120–125" (granite 108–132", quartz 120–126", minus 1–3" usable trim). **Any run >~120" requires a seam**, as do all L/U layouts and large islands. *Why: physical slab size limit.*

### 2.4 Waterfall & mitered edges (islands)

- **Waterfall = countertop continues vertically to the floor at the island end**, typically a **45° mitered joint** at the top corner. *Why: makes the top and leg read as one continuous slab.*
- **Miter must be cut at exactly 45° (≤¼° variance) and vein/grain book-matched across the corner.** *Why: the cascading pattern must flow unbroken across the joint; variance shows.*
- **Quartz is preferred for waterfalls** because engineered consistency makes vein-matching reliable. *Why: natural stone may require searching multiple slabs to flow the pattern.*

### 2.5 Backsplash height

- **Standard backsplash: 4" (range 3–6")** of the counter material. *Why: cheap protection of the wall at the counter line; budget-friendly.*
- **Full-height backsplash: counter to bottom of uppers (≈18"), or to the ceiling on open walls.** Use when: behind a range/hood as a focal wall, with premium counter material, or for an easy-clean seamless modern look. *Why: becomes a focal point and a continuous wipeable surface.*

### 2.6 Edge profiles & thickness

- **Standard thickness: 1.25" (3 cm).** 2 cm tops get a built-up 1.5" eased/ogee edge to read as thicker. *Why: 3 cm is the durable default; the build-up disguises thin material.*
- **Eased edge** = default modern; **bullnose/ogee** = traditional/high-end; **mitered build-up** = thick "slab" look. *Why: edge profile sets the style register.*

## ENGINE IMPLEMENTATION — Codifiable Rules

### Upper-cabinet alignment
1. **Default edge-stacking:** `upper.left_edge == base_below.left_edge AND upper.right_edge == base_below.right_edge` wherever a base exists below. Snap upper widths to the same 3" module as bases.
2. **Override hierarchy when edge-stacking conflicts** (priority high→low): (a) center upper/window on the sink or range focal point; (b) keep uppers flanking the hood equal width; (c) keep the upper-row bottom line continuous; (d) only then match base edges. If forced to break alignment, break it on the BASES, never on the uppers.
3. **Equal flank rule:** `width(upper_left_of_hood) == width(upper_right_of_hood)` and both centered on the hood centerline; hood width ≥ cooktop width + 6".
4. **Centering rule:** upper (or window) centerline = sink centerline; enforce equal reveal from each window/cabinet edge.
5. **Bottom-line constraint:** all standard uppers in a run share `bottom_AFF = 54"` (i.e., 18" above a 36"+1.5" counter). Parameterize `clearance = 18"` default; allow 15–24" override.
6. **Over-appliance lift:** over sink set bottom 30" above sink; over cooktop set bottom 30" (electric) / 36" (gas) above cooktop; recompute that cabinet's height so its TOP matches the run's top line.
7. **Top-line constraint for mixed heights:** choose one datum per run. For stacked-to-ceiling runs, lock the top line; for single rows, lock the bottom line. Never let both top and bottom vary in the same run.
8. **Over-fridge cabinet:** depth = 24" (not 12"); width = fridge enclosure width; flush face with adjacent tall cabinets.
9. **Over-hood cabinet:** bottom_AFF in [66", 72"]; depth matched to flue.
10. **Ceiling→height table:** ceiling 96"→30" uppers+crown; 108"→42" uppers OR stack 36"+10–12" topper; ≥108–120"→stacked uppers to ceiling. Flag bare 30" uppers on ≥108" ceilings as a warning.
11. **Stacked-uppers trigger:** `if ceiling_height >= 108" then prefer stacked_uppers OR uppers_to_ceiling_with_crown`. Crown height scales: 108"→6–12", 120"→9–18".
12. **Glass/open-cabinet symmetry:** place glass/open units in mirror pairs at run ends or flanking a focal point; forbid placement directly adjacent to the cooktop.
13. **Reveal constant:** inset reveal = 1/8" on all sides; frameless gap = 3 mm; hold a single reveal value across the entire elevation.

### Countertop placement
14. **Front overhang:** 1.5" past cabinet face (allow 1–1.5"); side overhang 1–1.5"; back 0". Finished depth = box_depth + front_overhang (24 + 1.5 = 25.5").
15. **Seating overhang by height:** lookup `{30":18", 36":15", 42":12"}`. Flag any unsupported cantilever >12" as requiring corbel/bracket/steel.
16. **Seam exclusion zone:** no seam within 6" of any sink or cooktop cutout edge (hard constraint). Allowed exception: seam tangent to the BACK edge of a cooktop cutout.
17. **Seam support:** every seam must lie over a cabinet partition/rail (`seam_x` ∈ supported_x_set). Reject seams over open spans (sink base interior, DW).
18. **Seam preference order:** (1) inside corner of L/U, (2) front-of-sink line, (3) narrowest span; minimize total seam count; reject seams across the widest visible field.
19. **Seam necessity:** `if run_length > 120" OR layout in {L, U} OR island_length > 120"` then ≥1 seam required. Seam width ≤1/16"; lippage ≤1/32".
20. **Waterfall miter:** model as 45° mitered joint at island end-top corner; require vein/grain continuity flag; default material = quartz for vein-match feasibility.
21. **Backsplash:** default 4" same-material; switch to full-height (counter→bottom-of-uppers ≈18", or →ceiling) when behind range/hood focal wall or when flagged premium/modern.
22. **Thickness/edge:** default 1.25" (3 cm) with eased edge; build up 2 cm material to 1.5" if used; profile token in {eased, bullnose, ogee, mitered}.
